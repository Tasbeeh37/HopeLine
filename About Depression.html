what is depression?

Depression, or depressive disorder, is a mental disorder that is widely spread among both adults and teenagers. It involves losing interest in daily activities and a persistent feeling of sadness or emptiness for a long period of time. It differs from normal mood swings, as it can affect relationships and, in some cases, lead to suicidal thoughts and behaviors. There are several factors that may contribute to the development of depression:

1. Biological Factors:
Genetics may play a role in increasing the risk of developing depression, as well as neurotransmitter imbalances and hormonal changes.

2. Psychological Factors:
 Depression may be the result of facing abuse, traumatic experiences, and significant stress, as well as negative thought patterns or personality traits such as perfectionism or self-criticism.

3. Environmental Factors:
 Social isolation or loneliness, and substance abuse, could trigger depressive episodes.

4. Physical Health Factors:
 The risk of depression may be exacerbated by chronic illnesses and neurological conditions or intensified by treatments such as chemotherapy.

5. Seasonal Factors:
 Less exposure to sunlight or variations in day length can affect circadian cycles and result in seasonal depression.

Depression can occur from one or more factors. Understanding these factors helps in determining the appropriate care and treatment.

Symptoms

Depression differs in symptoms that can affect various aspects of life, such as behaviors, mood, and physical health. Depending on their intensity, these symptoms can disrupt daily activities, relationships, and overall wellbeing. Below is a summary of the symptoms:

1. Emotional Symptoms
 Constant feeling of emptiness, sadness, and hopelessness in life that reduces interest in socializing and contributes to a negative outlook on life.

2. Cognitive Symptoms
 Facing concentration difficulties and frequent negative thoughts that affect self-esteem and self-worth.

3. Physical Symptoms
 Extreme wight gain or loss, excessive sleeping or insomnia, as well as stomachaches, headaches, and chronic pain.

4. Behavioral Symptoms
 Increased irritability, lack of motivation, withdrawal from hobbies or social interactions, and noticeable slowing in speech or movement.

Types

Based on symptoms and contributing factors, a therapist or psychologist can identify the specific type of depression a patient is experiencing, as depression types differ in both symptoms and causes. Below are several common types of depression:

1. Major depression

Individuals that experience major depression may suffer from extreme weight changes, insomnia, extreme sadness or hopelessness, feeling worthless, and having thoughts about ending their life or self-harm.

2. Persistent Depressive Disorder
 PDD involves experiencing mild or moderate symptoms that last for 2 years or longer. Common symptoms include overeating or undereating, feeling unenergized, difficulty making decisions, and having low self-esteem.

3. Bipolar Disorder
 Bipolar Disorder or manic depression is experiencing high energy or up mood episodes to low or depressive episodes. When going through a low episode, individuals may experience symptoms similar to those of major depression.

4. Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder
 DMDD is a depressive disorder that primarily affects children and teenagers, it appears in tantrums, intense anger and frequent irritability. Unlike typical moodiness, these behaviors are persistent and difficult for the child or teenager to overcome, often leading to significant challenges at home and school.

5. Seasonal Affective Disorder
 SAD is facing major depression symptoms triggered by seasonal changes. It occurs mostly in winter as the days get shorter and people get less sunlight. These factors lead to hormonal imbalances leading to SAD.

6. Psychotic Depression
 Individuals that have psychotic depression face the same symptoms as major depression alongside psychotic symptoms such as hallucinations, delusion, and paranoia.
